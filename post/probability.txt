measure of the linear relationship between two variables.</p>\n<p>Correlation coefficient:</p>\n<div class=\"math math-display\">\\rho = \\frac{Cov(X, Y)}{\\sigma_X \\sigma_Y}</div>\n<p>Properties:</p>\n<ol>\n<li><span class=\"math math-inline\">-1 \\leq \\rho \\leq 1</span></li>\n<li><span class=\"math math-inline\">\\rho = 1</span> or <span class=\"math math-inline\">-1</span> indicates perfect linear relationship</li>\n<li><span class=\"math math-inline\">\\rho = 0</span> indicates no linear relationship (but doesn't rule out other types of relationships)</li>\n</ol>\n<h2>4. Limit Theorems and Sampling Distributions</h2>\n<h3>4.1 Law of Large Numbers (LLN)</h3>\n<h4>4.1.1 Weak Law of Large Numbers</h4>\n<p>For a sequence of i.i.d. random variables <span class=\"math math-inline\">X_1, X_2, ..., X_n</span> with <span class=\"math math-inline\">E[X_i] = \\mu</span>,</p>\n<div class=\"math math-display\">P(|\\bar{X}_n - \\mu| > \\varepsilon) \\to 0 \\text{ as } n \\to \\infty, \\text{ for any } \\varepsilon > 0</div>\n<p>Where <span class=\"math math-inline\">\\bar{X}_n</span> is the sample mean of <span class=\"math math-inline\">n</span> observations.</p>\n<h4>4.1.2 Strong Law of Large Numbers</h4>\n<div class=\"math math-display\">P(\\lim_{n \\to \\infty} \\bar{X}_n = \\mu) = 1</div>\n<p>The strong law implies that the sample mean almost surely converges to the expected value.</p>\n<h3>4.2 Central Limit Theorem (CLT)</h3>\n<p>If <span class=\"math math-inline\">X_1, X_2, ..., X_n</span> are i.i.d. random variables with <span \\mu}{\\sigma/\\sqrt{n}} \\xrightarrow{d} N(0,1) \\text{ as } n \\to \\infty</div>\n<p>Where <span class=\"math math-inline\">\\bar{X}_n</span> is the sample mean, and <span class=\"math math-inline\">N(0,1)</span> is the standard normal distribution.</p>\n<h3>4.3 Sampling Distributions</h3>\n<h4>4.3.1 Sampling Distribution of the Mean</h4>\n<p>For a population with mean <span class=\"math math-inline\">\\mu</span> and standard deviation <span class=\"math math-inline\">\\sigma</span>:</p>\n<ol>\n<li><span class=\"math math-inline\">E(\\bar{X}) = \\mu</span></li>\n<li><span class=\"math math-inline\">\\sigma_{\\bar{X}} = \\frac{\\sigma}{\\sqrt{n}}</span>, where <span class=\"math math-inline\">n</span> is the sample size</li>\n<li>If the population is normally distributed, <span class=\"math math-inline\">\\bar{X} \\sim N(\\mu, \\frac{\\sigma^2}{n})</span> for any sample size</li>\n<li>If the population is not normal but <span class=\"math math-inline\">n</span> is large (typically <span class=\"math math-inline\">n \\geq 30</span>), <span class=\"math math-inline\">\\bar{X}</span> is approximately normal by CLT</li>\n</ol>\n<h4>4.3.2
